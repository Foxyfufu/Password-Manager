What is Social Engineering Attack?

Cyber security social engineering attack is the act of executing malicious activities accomplished through human interactions. It often preys on human emotions such as greed, curiosity or anxiety to successfully trick people to make security mistakes. Recently, social engineering attacks are prevailing, and more people fall prey into these meticulously laid traps.

Scareware

Scareware is a tactic that manipulates victims into believing that they need to download or buy a software. Most often initiated by bombarding victims with false alarms and fictitious threats to make them think that their systems are infected with malware. Scareware goals can vary from selling these fake software or installing the actual damaging malware to steal private information.

Phishing

Phishing uses a disguised e-mail to trick the recipient into believing it came from a legitimate source. The sender e-mail is often masked with an address that looks real and contains attachments to malware or links to malicious sites. When the victim clicks on the link or attachment, malware will be installed or malicious website that looks legitimate asking for confidential information will open.

Pretexting

Pretexting is typically phase one of a broader scheme to extract information from a victim. It usually involves the attacker creating a good pretext (fabricated scenario) and impersonating a trusted individual or a person who is credible such as police offers or banks. They pretend to need sensitive information urgently for a critical task and give the victim no time to think.

How to avoid falling victim?

1. Verify sender identity
Although most phishing e-mails may appear real in the first glance, many loopholes can be seen after taking closer looks. Phishing e-mails can be masked with real extensions to impersonate colleagues or government officials. However, clicking into the e-mail will show the actual sender e-mail address which is usually a dummy address with random characters. Other ways to identify can also include a generic greeting or unofficial e-mail template.

2. Multi factor verification
Multi factor verification is a security technology that requires multiple forms of methods to authenticate the userâ€™s identity for a login or transaction. The forms of identification are usually from these three key factors: Knowledge (password), Possession (token) and Inherence (facial recognition). Hence, users should always set up multi factor verification as attackers are unable to access the information even if they know the password since other methods of verification are required.
